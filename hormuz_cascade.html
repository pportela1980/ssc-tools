The Strait of Hormuz closure —
a cascade network
A disruption is not an event. It is a signal entering a system — and what matters is not the signal itself but the topology it travels through. This map applies network science and systems dynamics to a live geopolitical crisis, making visible the second and third-order consequences that arrive late, look like something else, and are structurally determined long before anyone notices them. The percentages are not forecasts. They are honest statements about what we know, what we are inferring, and where expertise runs out.

Probability methodology Probabilities are structured expert judgements, not statistical forecasts. They are derived from: (1) confirmed real-time data as of late March 2026; (2) supply chain lag models from cited institutional sources; (3) historical analogues — 1973 oil shock, 2021 Suez blockage, 2022 Ukraine gas crisis; (4) network position — nodes with more confirmed upstream causes receive higher base probabilities. All probabilities should be read as: "given the strait remains closed for 2–4 weeks, what is the probability this consequence manifests within the stated time horizon?" Dollar system nodes use a 5–10 year window. The reinforcing loop reflects a structural coupling between concurrent geopolitical disruptions — each node in the loop carries its own probability, but the loop as a whole activates when even partial energy re-dependency occurs.

A note on "dollar collapse" vs "dollar hegemony erosion"
Why "erosion" not "collapse"
Dollar collapse implies rapid, total loss of reserve currency status — no credible mechanism exists for this in a 2–5 year window. The yuan is not freely convertible. The euro is fiscally fragmented. No alternative has depth comparable to US Treasury markets. What IS happening is a gradual structural shift: declining USD share in trade invoicing (BIS) and central bank reserves (Atlantic Council), and Gulf states exploring bilateral non-dollar settlement (Brookings). This is a 5–10 year process. The 45% probability reflects meaningful acceleration of this trend within a decade — not catastrophic collapse. Complexity science should be precise about the difference between tipping points (fast) and slow structural drift.
A network map is not a forecast. It is a topology of consequence — a way of seeing which effects are inevitable, which are probable, and which are still preventable. The question is not whether this crisis will affect you. It already has. The question is whether you are making decisions at the right timescale, in response to the right signal.
The reinforcing feedback loop
The pink links in the map trace something conventional analysis will miss entirely: two concurrent geopolitical disruptions that are structurally coupled through European energy dependency. Each feeds the other. The loop does not return to its starting point — it ratchets. Breaking it requires coordinated action across multiple nodes simultaneously. That is precisely what the current environment makes least likely.

Individuals & households The signals that reach your life arrive late and look like something else
By the time you notice this crisis in your grocery bill, the cause will be six months old and already forgotten by the news cycle. That is not an accident — it is the structure of the system. The fertilizer that did not arrive in March will become the wheat harvest that was smaller in September, which will become the bread price that is higher in October. Each link in that chain is separated by weeks or months. No single headline will connect them.

The same logic applies to your energy bill, your mortgage rate, and the availability of the laptop or medical device you might need next year. The Strait of Hormuz is not in the news you read today. But it is already in the prices you will pay.
1
Fuel costs are already higher and will remain elevated through summer regardless of diplomatic resolution. Any fixed-price energy contract available to you is worth examining now, before the lag closes.
2
Autumn food prices will rise structurally — not because of speculation but because the physical harvest is already compromised. This is not reversible by a ceasefire. Budget accordingly for Q3–Q4 2026.
3
Mortgage and rent pressure will persist longer than the crisis itself. Central banks facing stagflation cannot cut rates to ease housing costs. If you are mid-decision on property, the rate environment you were planning around has shifted.
4
Electronics and medical devices — if you have a deferred purchase, the 3–6 month semiconductor lag means late 2026 is a worse time to buy than now.
Businesses & institutions Your supply chain exposure is almost certainly not where you think it is
Most organisations will respond to this crisis by looking at their energy spend. That is the visible node. It is also the one every competitor is watching. The organisations that will be most surprised are those with second and third-order commodity dependencies they have never mapped — the manufacturer whose packaging resin comes from a Gulf petrochemical plant, the hospital whose MRI service contract assumes helium availability that no longer exists, the food company whose supplier's fertilizer costs are about to restructure their input prices for the next two growing seasons.

The network diagram you just looked at is, in its essentials, a supplier risk map. Most organisations have a version of this in their procurement systems — but it stops at tier one. This is a tier-two and tier-three event.
1
Map your commodity dependencies to layer two. Ask not just "what do we buy?" but "what do our suppliers buy?" Helium, urea, polypropylene, and LNG are in more supply chains than most procurement teams know.
2
The 6-month food price lag is an accounting event, not just a social one. If your organisation buys food at scale, the cost structure of autumn 2026 is already determined. Model it now.
3
Insurance and logistics costs carry a fear premium that will not disappear when the strait reopens. Renegotiate freight contracts and war-risk insurance clauses before the new baseline is accepted as normal.
4
Scenario planning must include a 6-month closure case. Most current analysis assumes resolution within weeks. The network structure suggests the economic consequences are largely independent of when the strait reopens — the harvest cycle does not wait.
Aid organisations & foundations The crisis that will require your resources is not the one currently in the news
Emergency funding follows visibility. The network map shows that the largest and most diffuse consequences — food insecurity across Southeast Asia, healthcare disruption from helium shortfalls, the slow erosion of smallholder farm viability in fertilizer-dependent economies — will arrive six to eighteen months later, when donor attention has moved on.

This is a structural feature of how aid systems work. The question is whether any actor has the mandate and the foresight to position resources now for consequences that are highly probable but not yet visible. The nodes in the months and year+ rings are not hypothetical. They are already determined by what is happening today.
1
SE Asia food system stress is a 3–6 month event, not a year-horizon one. The fertilizer planting window closes in May. WFP and FAO positioning in Indonesia, Thailand, and Vietnam should begin now, not when prices rise.
2
Healthcare system resilience in helium-dependent regions is an underfunded second-order risk. Elective MRI rationing will fall disproportionately on under-resourced health systems.
3
Fund what is central, not what is visible. In network terms: nodes with highest betweenness centrality are systemic connectors. Interventions at these nodes prevent multiple downstream consequences simultaneously. This is where foundations with longer time horizons have genuine comparative advantage over emergency funds.
4
The dollar system transition is a decade-long governance question that affects aid architecture directly — reserve currency composition, multilateral lending capacity, USD-denominated debt service for Global South countries. It belongs in your strategic foresight agenda now.
On reinforcing loops and the limits of single-crisis thinking
The reason the feedback loop in this map is dangerous is not its size but its self-reinforcing character. Each activation makes the next activation more likely. The loop does not return to its starting point. It ratchets.

What complexity science adds is not just the identification of the loop — any good analyst can trace it once it is pointed out. What it adds is the understanding that the loop cannot be managed from inside any single node. No energy minister, no central bank governor, no humanitarian coordinator controls enough of the loop to break it unilaterally. Breaking it requires coordinated action across nodes simultaneously. That kind of multi-node, multi-domain coordination is precisely what the current geopolitical environment makes least likely. That is the real finding.
